WEEK 04: RAPID PROTOTYPING

Assignment: Make and document a kinetic sculpture, include circuitry, control it with a breadboard, measure voltage, find current using Ohm's Law, and use the Microcontroller Analog.

For this assignment I decided to create a dolphin that will infinitely swim. I got this idea from one of the toys that my brother has in which the dolphin will start to swim if you move the wheels. I thought this was going to be somewhat simple but it turned out to be more complicated than I thought. For this assignment, I used the materials listed in the photo below which are a compass, scissors, a utility knife, tape, wooden sticks, a ruler, and a pencil. This was the first time that I created a kinetic sculpture and it turned out to be pretty fun. Throughout the process, I also learned how to use a motor and connect it to the Metro board as well as using a potentiometer to turn the motor.



To start this sculpture, I decided to learn how to make gears which became more complicated than it looked. I went surfing on the Internet, looking at some videos, and grabbing different materials which were cardboard, opaline paper, and normal paper. The first gear that I did was made from cardboard and I drew it freehandedly. It kind of work and it was a great first gear but there was still a long way to go. Using my printer, I decided to print a model of a gear that I found on the internet and later trace it onto the opaline paper. Then, using the scissors I cut the gear and thought that I was finished. With the help of the compass, I made a hole in the center in order for the motor to fit, but I first had to pass a wooden stick through the hole in order to make it the right size. I then proceeded to test it with the motor and the gear turned. Just to be sure, I also made a carboard gear from the template that I printed and repeated the same steps. I now had two functioning gears!



After having two gears, I decided to make a mechanism that would rotate the dolphin so that it appeared to be swimming. I used a wooden stick and a compass to make a whole in both gears away from the center and attached a wooden stick to it. I then cut little squares from cardboard that I attached to the wooden stick. This would function as a space so that later, I could create the little cardboard rectangle that would hold the dolphin. I used some glue that I found in order to stick the three pieces. After doing this, I turned on the motor again to see if it would work but the gears wouldn't turn and instead, the motor would, twisting all the wires. I didn't know what to do until I checked Sam's documentation and saw that she used a little 3d printed part that fits the motor. I found it on my kit and made a huge hole in the center of both gears and stuck that 3d printed part. By this time, I saw that the opaline gear couldn't hold so much weight so instead I decided to stick with the cardboard replica of it. After trying again, the mechanism worked and I was ready to move on to the circuitry part.



For the circuitry part of the assignment, I decided to use a potentiometer that would indicate the speed at which the motor had to turn. For this, I connected the potentiometer to the breadboard and to three analog ports. This time, I used 5 volts instead of 3.3 due to the use of a motor. I then connected the digital pin number 3 to the L9110 H Bridge Power Driver and that to the motor. I now had all the setup ready and just needed to code it. I went to look at the course's webpage and found a code that did exactly what I wanted. I had already tried this in class and it worked perfectly fine, so I used it. I plugged the Metro into my computer, uploaded the code, and tried it. The gear started to turn slowly and as I turned the potentiometer, it started to turn faster. However, I made a slight adjustment in which I would print the speed of the potentiometer to the Serial Monitor in Arduino IDE in order to notice how fast the motor was spining. Success!





void setup() {
  pinMode(3, OUTPUT);
  pinMode(A0, OUTPUT);  //This will be GND for the potentiometer
  pinMode(A4, OUTPUT);  //This will be 3.3V for the pot

  digitalWrite(A0, LOW);
  digitalWrite(A4, HIGH);
}

void loop() {
  int pot_value = analogRead(A2);   //pot wiper is on A2
  Serial.println(pot_value);
  int motor_speed = map(pot_value, 0, 1023, 255, 0); // map motor_level to pot_value so that zero corresponds to lowest speed
  analogWrite(3, motor_speed);
  delay(1);
}

Lastly, I used opaline paper to create the dolphin by tracing an outline and cutting it out. I decided to add some color and made the dolphin blue. However, I thought that it would look pretty boring for the dolphin to be swimming in the air so I used some of the opaline paper that I had left and created some waves. I colored the top part of the waves light blue and the rest a little darker to look like the top and bottom of the ocean. After doing, this, I needed a place to mount the gear and the motor so I made a little cardboard box with tape in order to hold each piece in place. However, I quickly ran into a problem which was fitting the motor since the wires had no place to connect to the L9110 H Bridge Power Driver so using the utility knife, I cut out a little hole in the back carboard piece and it was just the right size for the motor to pass through it. I now had everything ready so I turned on the motor using the potentiometer but something wasn't quite right.



When I turned the motor, the little cardboard stick that was were the dolphin goes started to tilt until it touched the floor. I decided to use some of the black spare wire that I had and made a little hole for the cardboard stick to go through. When I turned the motor back on, the stick ran smoothly and I was almost done with the assignment. I used the multimeter to measure the voltage of the potentiometer and the digital pin 3 which was connected to the L9110 H Bridge Power Driver. I got some pretty reasonable measurements. I then proceeded to use Ohm's Law to find the current through the potentiometer. I turned the potentiometer until it was about halfway in its path and measured the voltage. The voltage was 3.26 volts but the resistance was more difficult to find. After searching on the Internet, I found that the potentiometer has somewhere the resistance printed on it. I found that it was 10k Ohm's. Knowing this, I used Ohm's Law and found that the current was 0.000326 amps. After doing this calculation, the only thing that was left to do was tape the waves and the dolphin and there it was, my first kinetic sculpture!
